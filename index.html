My Train Rides Over Time

Missouri River Eagle

The Missouri River Eagle passenger train, run by the Missouri Pacific Railroad, still existed by the summer of 1967. It ran between the St. Louis, MO Union Station and the Kansas City Union Station.

In 1967, this train had only a couple of cars and offered no amenities such as food or drink service. It was typically powered by one of GM's E series passenger engines.

Union Pacific Portland Rose

Union Pacific's Portland Rose passenger survived for almost forty years, originally running from Chicago to Portland, OR.

In 1954, the name was assigned to a new origin, Kansas City, MO, but still terminated in Portland. It survived until Amtrak took over in 1971.

In 1967, in contrast to the Missouri River Eagle, this train retained many of the amenities typical of American passenger trains in their glory days: attractive equipment (cars) painted in Union Pacific armour yellow, dining cars with appetizing menus and comfortable, reclining seats (coach!) with more than twice the legroom of today's domestic, first- class airline seating.

Like the Missouri Pacific Eagle, the Portland Rose in 1967 utilized GM's E series passenger engines.

California Zephyr

The California Zephyr retained its identity, route and appearance essentially unchanged since its inception in 1949 until its takeover by Amtrak in 1971. It ran from Chicago to Oakland, California, across the bay from San Francisco.

It differed from the Missouri River Eagle and the Portland Rose in that it was jointly sponsored by three different railroads: the Chicago, Burlington and Quincy; the Denver and Rio Grande Western and the Western Pacific. Advertised as "the most talked about train in the country", it ran along what is arguably the most scenic route in the U.S., passing over historic old railroad bridges spanning the Mississippi and Missouri rivers and through the heart of both the Rocky Mountains and the Sierra Nevadas.

The California Zephyr utilized what most people remember as the most prestigious passenger equipment ever to run during passenger train travel's heyday in the 1940's and 1950's. The cars, manufactured by both the Budd and Pullman Companies, were of fluted stainless steel and carried the California Zephyr train name on their display boards, with the railroad owner's name stenciled in black on the upper side corner of the car. The most dramatic cars were the "bubble", or dome, cars, which allowed a 360 degree view from comfortable seats on the upper level over the roof. These cars variably served as coaches, sleepers (bedroom cars) and diners.

The only comparable train in prestige might be considered New York Central's 20th Centry Limited running between Chicago and New York.
